Module 1 · The Foundation

What Differentiation Really Means

Before building the practice, we clarify the mission — and examine the beliefs that determine whether the practice will actually work.

What Differentiation Actually Is

Differentiation is a teaching philosophy and a set of instructional decisions that ensure all students have access to grade-level learning goals through varied entry points, representations, scaffolds, and supports. The destination — the standard, the concept, the grade-level goal — remains the same for every student. What changes is the pathway.

Rigor defines the destination. Differentiation ensures every student has an accessible pathway to reach it. These two ideas are not in conflict — they depend on each other.

✓ What It Is

  • Adjusting entry points, not goals
  • Varying representation and scaffolding
  • More intensive support for students furthest behind
  • A continuous instructional response
  • Every student reaching grade-level thinking

✗ What It Is Not

  • Separate, easier work for "low" students
  • Permanently fixed ability groups
  • Reducing the mathematical goal
  • More problems for advanced students
  • A one-time accommodation
The Equity Argument

When we teach every student the same way at the same pace, we are not treating them equally — we are treating them identically, which is not the same thing. True equity means every student gets what they need to access the same high-quality content.

What Believing All Students Can Learn Actually Means

"All students can learn" is one of the most frequently stated and least operationalized beliefs in education. Beliefs are only real when they change behavior. Here is what it looks like when teachers genuinely hold this belief:

  • They seek out what a student CAN do before focusing on what they cannot
  • They maintain grade-level goals even when scaffolding is necessary
  • They provide the most intensive instruction to students with the most significant gaps
  • They refuse to let a student's past performance become a prediction for their future
  • They examine their own instructional choices when students are not progressing
  • They communicate high expectations directly and consistently to every student

What This Looks Like Across Grade Levels

K–2 · Primary
Classroom Scenario Teaching Addition Within 20
Three kindergarteners are working on the same addition standard. All three are working toward the same goal: understanding that numbers can be combined to make a larger quantity.
Foundational group Uses red and blue counters on a ten-frame to physically build 6 + 4. Touches each counter while counting. "Show me 6 red bears. Now add 4 blue bears. How many altogether?"
On-grade group Uses a drawn number line. Starts at 6, makes 4 hops. Records the equation. "Start at 6. Take 4 jumps. Where do you land?"
Extension group Works with equation cards. Writes related equations (6+4=10, 4+6=10). Explores: Can you find another way to make 10?
Key Principle for K–2

All three groups are working on the same addition concept. The goal never changed — only the representation. This is differentiation, not three different lessons.

Grades 3–5 · Upper Elementary
Classroom Scenario Teaching Fraction Concepts (Grade 4)
All students are working toward the same standard: understanding equivalent fractions and comparing fractions with different denominators.
Foundational group Uses fraction strips to compare 1/2 and 3/4 physically. "Lay your 1/2 strip next to your 3/4 strip. Which is longer? How do you know?"
On-grade group Uses area models drawn on grid paper. Shades fractions and compares. Begins finding common denominators with a visual support.
Extension group Works symbolically. Generates equivalent fractions, places on a number line, and explains: "Why does multiplying numerator and denominator by the same number keep the value equal?"
Key Principle for 3–5

In upper elementary, differentiation shifts toward questioning depth. All groups answer the same essential question — but the DOK level of what's expected varies from recall to strategic thinking.

Secondary · Grades 6–12
Classroom Scenario Teaching Linear Equations (Algebra I)
All students are working toward understanding slope as rate of change in context — the same standard, the same essential concept.
Foundational group Uses a table of values with a real-world context (cost per mile for a rideshare). "How much does the cost go up each mile? That change is the slope." Uses algebra tiles to connect to y = mx + b.
On-grade group Given a graph and equation. Interprets slope in context, calculates from two points, connects to the equation.
Extension group Compares multiple linear relationships. "Which rate of change is steeper? What does that mean in this context? Write a scenario where this would matter."
Key Principle for Secondary

Secondary differentiation is often about context and questioning — not different content. Every student learns slope. What varies is the sophistication of the reasoning expected and the support provided to get there.

Module 2 · The Mirror

Bias, Belief & Teacher Mindset

The most sophisticated instructional strategy cannot overcome a teacher who doesn't believe in a student. This module asks you to look inward.

How Bias Shows Up in Math Instruction

Bias in mathematics teaching is often invisible — not because teachers are malicious, but because bias operates below conscious awareness. It shows up in instructional decisions made dozens of times per day.

  • Consistently calling on certain students during number talks and ignoring others
  • Assigning less rigorous tasks to students based on race, poverty, or language background
  • Interpreting quiet or disengaged students as "not math people"
  • Using summative data from the beginning of year as a permanent label
  • Framing ELL students or students with IEPs as incapable rather than not-yet
  • Assuming family structure or home environment limits learning potential
Research Finding

Stereotype threat — the psychological burden of being at risk of confirming a negative stereotype — is a documented barrier to mathematical performance. When teachers hold and communicate negative stereotypes, even subtly, students internalize them.

Fixed vs. Growth Mindset in Teachers

Carol Dweck's research on mindset applies as much to teachers as to students. The language we use in our heads becomes the instruction we deliver.

Fixed Mindset Language

  • "He just can't do fractions — he's never been strong in math."
  • "My low kids can't handle this concept yet."
  • "She's just not a math person."
  • "I've tried everything with him."
  • "Some kids are just wired differently."

Growth Mindset Language

  • "She hasn't connected fractions to division yet. What experience would bridge that?"
  • "These students need a concrete representation first."
  • "He hasn't mastered this yet — what am I missing?"
  • "What support haven't I tried?"
  • "Every student can develop mathematical understanding."

A Weekly Self-Reflection Protocol

  • Which students did I call on most this week? Which ones least?
  • Which students did I pull for small groups? Which ones did I not reach?
  • When a student struggled, what story did I tell myself about why?
  • Did I maintain grade-level goals for every student, or did I quietly reduce expectations for some?
  • What would it look like to give my most struggling student my most thoughtful instruction?

Interrupting Bias Across Grade Levels

K–2 · Primary
Watch ForBias in Primary Math Instruction
In primary grades, bias often shows up in who gets to use manipulatives "freely" vs. who is told they "need" them, and in how we respond when young children struggle.
Interrupting it Track who you place in your daily small group. Are the same children always there? Use exit slips and observation notes — not your memory or general impression — to make grouping decisions. Young children's understanding shifts rapidly; yesterday's data is already old.
Grades 3–5 · Upper Elementary
Watch ForAbility Labels That Stick
Upper elementary is where ability labels start to calcify. Students begin to identify as "not a math person" — often because their teacher's behavior toward them communicated that message.
Interrupting it Review the tasks you've given your lowest-performing group this month. Are they genuinely grade-level tasks with scaffolding — or have you quietly reduced the cognitive demand? Task design is where bias most often hides in Grades 3–5.
Secondary · Grades 6–12
Watch ForTracking as Institutionalized Bias
Secondary tracking is the most entrenched form of educational bias in mathematics. Course placement decisions are frequently influenced by factors other than mathematical readiness — and they follow students for years.
Interrupting it When a secondary student says "I'm just not a math person," push back directly: "That's not a real thing. Math is a skill, not a trait. Let's figure out what you haven't learned yet and fix that." The research is clear — fixed math identity is taught, not innate.

Module 3 · The Framework

Guided Math Across Grade Levels

Guided Math is a structure for organizing your math block so you can deliver targeted small-group instruction while the rest of your students work independently — at every grade level from kindergarten through high school.

Why Whole-Group Instruction Alone Is Not Enough

In any classroom, students arrive at different points of understanding. A whole-group lesson taught to "the middle" simultaneously leaves struggling students behind and under-challenges advanced students. The Guided Math framework solves this by creating dedicated time each day when you work with the students who need you most, in the way they need you.

Guided Math Across Grade Levels

K–2 · Primary
StructureWhat Guided Math Looks Like in K–2
In primary grades, Guided Math is fundamentally concrete. Young children build mathematical understanding through physical experience with objects. The small group setting ensures every child has manipulatives in hand.
Group size & frequency 3–5 students per group. 12–18 minutes each. Highest-need students seen EVERY day. On-grade students 3–4x per week. Advanced students 1–2x per week for depth.
Building independence Week 1: Teach center routines whole-class only. Week 2: Add a second center, practice transitions. Week 3: Pull your first small group (8–10 min max). Kindergarten: Don't pull groups until week 4 or 5.
What centers look like Hands-on, concrete tasks tied to current content. Math games with manipulatives. Journal prompts with draw-and-explain. Students should be able to run them independently and productively.

Grades 3–5 · Upper Elementary
StructureWhat Guided Math Looks Like in Grades 3–5
In upper elementary, students can handle richer mathematical conversations and more complex independent tasks. Groups meet longer and can explore concepts more deeply.
Group size & frequency 4–6 students per group. 15–20 minutes each. Students can sustain independent work for 30–40 minutes with meaningful tasks.
What independent tasks look like Collaborative problem-solving with accountability structures. Math journals with strategy explanations. Partner games with written recording. Spiral review tasks. Exit ticket completion.
Differentiation within small groups One lesson plan with three scaffold levels. Foundational group: concrete/pictorial representation. On-grade: pictorial/abstract. Extension: abstract reasoning and application. All working toward the same standard.

Secondary · Grades 6–12
StructureWhat Guided Math Looks Like in Secondary
Secondary Guided Math faces structural challenges: 50-minute periods, departmentalized content, whole-class direct instruction culture. But the need for small-group instruction doesn't diminish — it intensifies, because unaddressed gaps compound.
The conference pull model Instead of traditional centers, use structured independent practice time to create 15–20 minutes for small groups. 5–7 minute check-ins with targeted groups while class works. 2–3 groups per week based on specific skill data.
Groups are concept-specific A student may need foundational support for fractions but perform at or above grade level in geometry. Secondary groups are built on this week's formative data — not a course-level ability label.
Reframe the objection "I don't have time for small groups" → You don't have time NOT to. When a student doesn't understand a concept, re-teaching it to the whole class takes more time than a targeted 10-minute conversation. Small groups are time recovery, not time cost.
