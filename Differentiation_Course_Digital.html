"Every child can learn. Every child deserves a teacher who believes it and acts on it."
Differentiation is not remediation.
It's keeping every student moving toward the same high-quality mathematical goal through varied pathways — not lowering expectations for some and raising them for others.
Bias shows up in instruction.
Which students we call on, who we pull for small groups, what tasks we assign — teacher beliefs shape every one of these decisions, often without awareness.
Small groups aren't optional.
Whole-group instruction alone cannot serve a room where students arrive at vastly different points. Targeted small-group time is the mechanism that closes gaps.

The Framework: Guided Math Across Grade Levels
What it looks like from kindergarten through high school

Guided Math is not a one-size-fits-all framework. A kindergarten classroom running centers looks completely different from a secondary math period using conference pulls. This module shows you what Guided Math looks like — and how to make it work — at every level.

Why whole-group instruction alone is insufficient
K–2: Building independence before pulling groups
Grades 3–5: Richer conversations, deeper reasoning
Secondary: 50-minute periods and conferencing models
Scheduling templates for 60- and 75-minute blocks
Group frequency targets by learner need
"I don't have time for small groups" → Reframe: You don't have time NOT to. Small groups are time recovery, not time cost.
4
The Lens: CPA & Shifts in Mathematical Learning
Understanding how students build mathematical knowledge

The Concrete–Pictorial–Abstract progression is one of the most powerful differentiation tools available. All three groups can work toward the same goal at the same time — with the teacher asking the same conceptual questions — while each group works at the representational stage that matches their current understanding.

Jerome Bruner and the CPA framework
CPA as a differentiation tool, not a grade-level filter
How CPA applies to secondary mathematics
Recognizing "shifts" in student thinking in real time
DOK levels as a differentiation lever
Number talk questioning across three scaffold levels
CPA is not a one-way street. Students at every grade level — including high school — benefit from returning to concrete and pictorial representations. This is responsive teaching, not regression.

Practical tools for a sustainable differentiation practice

The biggest barrier to differentiation is usually not motivation — it's time and information. This module gives you a complete data collection toolkit, a flexible grouping system, and a sustainable weekly planning approach that takes no more than 20 minutes once established.

Clipboard coding: daily observational data in 2 minutes
Exit tickets that actually inform next-day instruction
Math interviews: the gold standard of formative assessment
Why static ability grouping is instructional malpractice
How often groups should change (by grade band)
What students do while you pull groups
Using a beginning-of-year screener as the primary grouping basis all year is a form of malpractice. Students change — sometimes dramatically — in weeks. Groups built on old data serve old versions of students.

Core Framework · Module 4

The Concrete–Pictorial–Abstract Progression

All three groups work toward the same mathematical goal — at the representational stage that matches their current understanding.

C
Concrete
Physical Objects
Students work directly with manipulatives they can touch and move. The math lives in the objects themselves.
Fraction tiles, counters on a ten-frame, base-ten blocks, algebra tiles. "Build it first."
P
Pictorial
Visual Representations
Students work with drawings, diagrams, number lines, and bar models. The object becomes an image.
Number line jumps, area models, bar models, drawn arrays. "Draw what you know."
A
Abstract
Symbols & Notation
Students work with numbers and symbols without physical or visual support. The idea is internalized.
Equations, algorithms, symbolic notation, mental math. "Prove it with symbols."
Malpractice vs. Effective Practice

Honest Mirrors
for Reflective Teachers.

These contrasts run throughout the course — not as judgment, but as the kind of honest professional self-check most PD doesn't have the courage to offer.

⚠ Malpractice
✗ Grouping students as "low/middle/high" and leaving them there all year
✗ Relying on a September screener as permanent grouping data
✗ Pulling a small group without planning or anticipating student moves
✗ Letting preconceived notions drive instructional decisions
✗ Advanced students never seen because "they're fine"
✗ Teaching differentiation without planning — just winging it
✓ Effective Practice
✓ Groups built on current data about this week's specific concept
✓ Multiple data sources inform each grouping decision
✓ Launching learning with an activation, teaching concretely, checking for understanding
✓ Recognizing student strengths as a catalyst for addressing needs
✓ Advanced students seen weekly for depth and investigation
✓ Planning content, vocabulary, tools, and varied DOK levels simultaneously
Guided Math Across Grade Levels

One Framework.
Three Contexts.

Primary
Kindergarten – Grade 2
Groups of 3–5 students, 12–18 minutes each
Concrete learning is non-negotiable — every student has manipulatives in hand
Highest-need students seen every single day
Centers must run independently — teach the routines first
Kindergarten: don't pull groups until week 4 or 5
Assessment via observation, interviews, brief exit slips
Upper Elementary
Grades 3 – 5
Groups of 4–6 students, 15–20 minutes each
Deeper mathematical conversations; richer reasoning tasks
One lesson plan, three scaffold levels (C, P, A)
DOK variation keeps all groups at grade-level thinking
Exit tickets and journal review drive next-group decisions
Advanced learners: extension through depth, not next grade's content
Secondary
Grades 6 – 12
Conference pulls: 5–7 min check-ins during independent work
Structure independent practice time to free the teacher
Groups are concept-specific, not student-level labels
CPA still matters: algebra tiles, number lines unlock understanding
Meet 2–3x per week based on specific formative data
Small groups are time recovery — not time cost

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions Teachers Ask.

Is this course really for secondary teachers too? +
Yes — genuinely. Module 3 has a dedicated section on secondary Guided Math that addresses the 50-minute period, conference pull models, and how to free up small-group time during independent practice. Module 4 shows how CPA applies to algebra, geometry, and higher math. The need for differentiation doesn't decrease as students get older — it compounds.
What if my school just adopted a new curriculum? Is this still relevant? +
Module 6 was written specifically for this situation. It walks through how to use a new curriculum's built-in scaffolds, how to use small-group time to address what the curriculum misses for specific students, and how to advocate constructively when curriculum pacing and student readiness are misaligned.
How is this different from other differentiation PD I've done? +
Most differentiation PD either stays at the philosophy level (lots of theory, few tools) or jumps straight to strategies without addressing the mindset work underneath them. This course does both — and it includes a module on bias and teacher belief that most PD doesn't have the courage to include. It also specifically addresses Guided Math structures across every grade band, which is rarely covered in a single resource.
Can I use this to lead PD for my team? +
The school/team license (available on Gumroad) includes a facilitator guide with module-by-module discussion questions, a team commitment protocol, and notes for leading each section. It covers up to 30 educators and is designed for instructional coaches, math coaches, and teacher leaders.
How long does the course take to complete? +
Each module takes approximately 45–60 minutes to read deeply and complete the reflection activities — about 5–6 hours total for the full course. Most teachers find it most effective to do one module per week over six weeks, reflecting on and implementing each strategy before moving to the next. That said, it's completely self-paced — you can go faster or slower.
Is this aligned to Common Core or Texas TEKS? +
Both. The course itself is standards-agnostic in its framework — the principles of CPA, flexible grouping, and equity-centered differentiation apply regardless of your state's standards. The companion lesson resources in the project (sold separately) are available in both CCSS and Texas TEKS editions.
